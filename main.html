The End of the Beginning

4 May 2016

Twelve weeks flew by so quickly and it all culminated with a presentation night in front of 60+ The Iron Yard staff, employers, recruiters, friends and family. During presentation night, a senior front-end developer asked me how my experience was during final projects, and I candidly admitted it was frustrating and fun. I almost regretted saying that, but to be fair, that is how I truly felt. The frustration of a difficult project does not outweigh the rewards; it is what fuels your passion and keeps you focused on hitting MVP. The key is to have a positive outlook through all of it. In the end, I was proud of what our team had built together. After 2.5 weeks, we shipped our app, Rider. We felt like developers.

Being a part of blended team of two front-end developers (including myself) and four rails developers was a great experience. We all worked so well together, and each one of my teammates contributed greatly and left a lasting impression of the project. I want to give a shout-out to the back-end team who worked hard to build the open source, fully documented API that we integrated, appropriately named “Farefairy."

Side note: Funny enough, my birthday fell on the date of our big presentation night. One teammate, who I won’t name (but may or may not go by the name of Jefferson), brought in cupcakes to celebrate. Did I mention I worked with the best team ever?

Pitch Perfect

7 April 2016

Last week was our last week of lecture, and it ended with "Pitch Day" for final projects on Friday. Many of our classmates prepared pitches for a website or app they were passionate about and would like to see in production. The final project is a cross-collaboration project, consisting of a large team of Ruby on Rails developers and Front-End developers. A fellow cohort member and I pitched "Rider" app together on Friday, and I am thrilled to say that it was picked. Our incredible team is now hard at work on making this concept a reality! Being a team-lead is a big undertaking, but I couldn't be more humbled and excited about this opportunity.

Our first sprint ran from this past Monday to today, and in just a few short days we were able to hit MVP by building out an AngularJS app calling from an API crafted by our back-end team. AngularJS is still intimidating, and sometimes I stare at my screen wondering why I'm getting these errors. It's kind of funny to think that just a few weeks ago, I dreaded getting errors in the console. Now when I see them, I find myself saying "Good! That narrows it down." That's the kind of attitude that will get you through any blocker you have. Googling doesn't hurt either!

If you're curious enough to see daily sneak peaks and little moments of victory (that may of may not be dancing that we record on video), I created a twitter for Rider. Follow @rider_app for updates on our progress throughout these next few weeks.

Dear Future Bootcampers

17 March 2016

Today there was a meeting for prospective students at my campus. I met a few curious minds who were eager to hear about my experience and my peers experiences before and during this process. It had occured to me that I started this blog without mentioning why I decided to go down this journey in the first place. I thought I should share my own experience starting from my decision to go into a bootcamp and choosing the right one for me.

Why Bootcamp? Everyone has their own reason as to why they want to go to bootcamp, whether it be for personal enrichment, a complete career change, or using skills they acquire here for their own startup. Whatever it it may be, choosing the right bootcamp is one's own personal journey. My personal reason: career change. The previous company I had worked for had contracted a company to develop an app for them. Being able to create it myself, rather than just speak about what I thought it should do and have someone else interpret it, seemed much more interesting to me. Like many others, I explored this interest though meetups, weekend and night classes and online tutorials. However, I wanted a career change and real world experience with developers. Bootcamp seemed like the next step into my newfound career, a decision I am glad I made.

Which Bootcamp? When I was "bootcamp shopping", I utilized resources like Course Report and SwitchUp. Those were great, but seeing the bootcamps in person, meeting the instructors, and getting feedback from current students or alumni were invaluable in making my decision. This is an investment in your time and money, and you want to make sure it's worth it. What I've found is that generally bootcamps across the country have similar curriculum and a lot of the same philosophies. But the people behind it such as the instructors, administrators, and even board members make all the difference. You want to know who is teaching you and how they will be teaching. These past few weeks have been exceptionally difficult, but also equally enriching and fulfilling. I don't know that I would have learned this much or made it this far if it weren't for my instructor. During the bootcamp interview process, interview your instructors and make sure they are a great fit for you too.

Good luck on your bootcamp journey!

Coffee and JavaScript

05 March 2016

A few weeks ago, I started bootcamp. Not a "bootcamp" in the traditional sense, but most definitely a bootcamp. For the past 5 weeks, The Iron Yard Academy has been my home, mostly because i've been all but slept here these past few weeks, but partly because these people end up becoming your fellow wonderfully nerdy, cat-meme loving family. So what have I struggled through and what have I done so far? Well, let me put this in an unordered list for you.

  • HTML: the skeleton, the king! I enjoy how literal it is. It has been a great foray into coding and after a few weeks of practicing, I feel as though this hypertext markup language and I are now best friends. I also enjoy using geolocation in HTML5 and one day I hope to feel the same for localStorage. One day.
  • CSS: Cascading Style Sheets is both beautiful and beautifully hacky. I enjoy making web pages centered, adding media queries in and looking at how responsive my work can be, and playing around with it's simpler but sleeker sister pre-processer, Sass. We also looked into Twitter's popular framework, Bootstrap. Due to my time constraints, and the fact that I would like to get back to doing my assignment, I used Bootstrap to create this quick and easy blog in just a few minutes!
  • JavaScript: Ah, this is truly the heart of the course. We've become familiar with Vanilla JavaScript; from functions, methods and objects, arrays and loops, we have practiced them all. The daily coding challenges can be intimidating, but they are great practice. Then, we learned jQuery and wondered what we have been doing all our lives. jQuery, I love you.

For those of you looking for some great resources for newbies, I highly recommend the following:

  • HTML&CSS by Jon Duckett
  • JAVASCRIPT&JQUERY by Jon Duckett
  • Eloquent Javascript Second Edition
